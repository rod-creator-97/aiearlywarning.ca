The Problem

Macro Stability Is Hiding Micro Disruption

Aggregate employment numbers continue to look healthy. Beneath them, specific cohorts of early-career workers in AI-exposed occupations are already losing ground. The instruments Canada uses to monitor the labour market were not designed to see this divergence.

The System

Four Data Streams for Early Detection

Each data stream answers a different question. Together they form a layered monitoring architecture that can detect displacement signals before they appear in national statistics. The system should be built on domestic data first, with frontier lab telemetry treated as a plug-in upgrade.

01
AI Usage Statistics
Near-real-time visibility into how AI models are being used to automate or augment work tasks. The automation-augmentation classification has been empirically validated as a predictor of employment outcomes. Open-weight models account for 20 to 30% of total AI usage and generate no telemetry when run on private hardware.
02
Dynamic Task Exposure Metrics
Assessments of how susceptible each occupation is to AI automation, re-scored as model capabilities change. Can be produced internally by TechStat using frontier models to score the full National Occupational Classification, with no external data agreements required.
03
High-Frequency Payroll Data
Confirms whether displacement has reached actual workers through near-real-time hiring and separation signals. Commercial providers cover an estimated 80% of the Canadian workforce at the occupation-by-age-by-province granularity the Labour Force Survey cannot produce.
04
Firm-Level AI Adoption Surveys
Helps attribute observed employment changes to AI rather than to other economic forces. Canada's only high-frequency source (the CSBC) has its final collection cycle scheduled for August 2026. A replacement must be secured before that data stream disappears.
Key Findings

What the Analysis Reveals

Six findings drawn from the analysis. Together they describe the emerging displacement signal and the gaps in Canada's ability to detect and respond to it.

01
The Canary Cohort
Roughly 66,705 early-career workers sit inside seven occupations that already show measurable AI-driven contraction. Two-thirds of them work in Ontario and Quebec.
02
The Coverage Gap
Open-weight models account for an estimated 20 to 30% of all AI usage, and when users run these models on their own hardware, no usage statistics are generated. Any system built solely on frontier lab data is structurally blind to this segment.
03
The Single-Lab Skew
Coding-task share differs by a factor of nine between Claude and ChatGPT. Relying on one provider's data as ground truth creates a distorted picture of what work AI is absorbing.
04
Canada's Survey Gaps
Labour Force Survey cells are too small to detect cohort-level shocks, and the Census operates on a five-year cycle. Neither was designed for a labour market that can shift in months.
05
CERB's Warning
$4.6 billion in confirmed overpayments traced to the inability to verify employment status in real time. The next disruption will arrive faster than the program design cycle that produced CERB.
06
The EI Eligibility Gap
Employment Insurance requires prior insurable hours. Young workers entering the labour market for the first time fall outside it, and they are the cohort most exposed to an AI-driven hiring freeze.

Recommendations

Six Recommendations for Mila and the Government of Canada

For Mila
1 Brief TechStat on the indicator framework in this report and advocate for the data streams it will need, engaging in Q2 2026 before TechStat's first-year work plan is finalized.
2 Convene a Canadian AI labour displacement scenario exercise in October or November 2026 that produces a scenario-to-policy matrix mapping plausible displacement scenarios to candidate policy responses.
3 Consult provinces through bilateral outreach to Ontario and Quebec and a joint technical briefing with the Labour Market Information Council, and communicate Canada's data requirements to the international AI usage data standard effort.
For the Government of Canada
4 TechStat should secure three domestic data streams in 2026: an LFS digital platform supplement at quarterly frequency, absorption of the CSBC AI adoption module before its August 2026 sunset, and voluntary data-sharing partnerships with ADP Canada and Dayforce.
5 TechStat and ESDC's Labour Market Information Directorate should begin quarterly calibration sessions in mid-2026 to develop shared signal interpretation, escalation thresholds, and signal-to-response mapping.
6 ESDC should table two sequenced items at the FLMM's Changing Nature of Work and Skills Working Group: an awareness agenda item in late 2026 and a structured working session on means-testing treatment of future AI displacement benefits in mid-2027.

The full report includes detailed implementation plans, timelines, and named counterparts for each recommendation.
